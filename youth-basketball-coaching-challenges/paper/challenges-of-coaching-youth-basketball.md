## Abstract

This study identifies the challenges that are present for youth basketball coaches in hopes of improving the current pre-season trainings offered by the City of Santa Clarita. It is vital to know that the City does more than most cities as far as orienting their coaches for the upcoming season. There are about three trainings that all coaches have to complete. Along with these trainings, I felt that there was not enough being done to prepare coaches for the upcoming challenges present in coaching youth basketball. First was the priority to find what challenges coaches are currently facing as viewed by the coaches in the recreation league. Presumably, the first thought is that parents are the biggest challenge for coaches. Further research was needed to either debunk or support that statement, which we look to understand from this study. After surveying, qualitative analysis was conducted in the form of five interviews with coaches involved in the program. The respondents in the interviews had been selected by indicating that they want to be involved further in the research. Through qualitative analysis a different collection of challenges was indicated through the surveys. These results help support the research question of identifying the challenges in coaching youth basketball.

## Introduction

Due to my job responsibility of recruiting and retaining volunteer youth sports coaches for the City of Santa Clarita, I had decided to conduct a deeper analysis of the issues revolving around coaching youth basketball. Additionally, I also volunteer as a youth basketball coach for a non-profit travel basketball organization for players between the ages of seven and eighteen years of age. Currently, there are resources available to youth coaches to help prepare them for coaching basketball, but I felt that there was a possibility that they were inefficient and training methods were failing to prepare coaches for the daunting tasks ahead of them. As a self-proclaimed perfectionist and basketball coach on the administrative side of our sports organization for the City of Santa Clarita, I felt that more analysis was needed to understand the challenges youth basketball coaches face to better impact the overall satisfaction for the coaches involved.

**Research question:** What are the challenges in coaching youth basketball?

## Review of Literature

### How Parents are the Biggest Presumed Issue for Youth Coaches

A common conflict that is present in all youth sports is the involvement of overbearing parents and their interactions that may discourage positive benefits of sport participation. Parents may influence their children to neglect the purposeful coaching instruction presented by the coach. Arthur-Banning, Wells, Barber, and Hegreness (2009) have found that parents' behavior at youth basketball games are correlated with the positive and negative experiences that players may deal with when engaged in sport participation (p. 14). If this is true for the players, then it should be noted that the social behaviors of parents can also influence the attitude of the coach due to their interactions throughout the course of the coaching experience. Also, Arthur-Banning et al. (2009) emphasized that parents' communication with a child participant can negatively conflict with the player-coach relationship (p. 12). If conflicting perspectives are experienced, this forces the player to take a stance against the coach in order to please the parent. Baltzell et al. (2014) used an online survey designed for high school coaches looking to understand the challenges that these coaches faced and how that causes them unhappiness. Baltzell et al. (2014) found that issues with parents at the high school level were very prevalent towards affecting the overall mood of the coaches.

### Are Parents' Attitudes Ultimately Causing the Demise of Youth Basketball Coaches?

The crucial role for the youth basketball sport programs is not only a mentoring role, but coaches also provide transportation, time, money, and organization for millions of children and adolescents (Wiersma & Sherman, 2005). The stressors that ultimately take a toll on the coach are numerous; one major stressor is that of the negative and overbearing behavior exhibited by the parents toward the coach. Unfortunately, this is a very common stressor widely exhibited, but it is also a preventable stressor. This is seen as a universal issue within youth basketball programs. Not only do the coaches express the weight of the normal obstacles that they must deal with — which include transportation, time, and money — but then throw in the negative overbearing parents that result in the child ultimately becoming the mirror of the parents. This directly affects the coach's overall ability to successfully lead and coach an entire team of young individuals, but why would a coach want to work within a hostile environment (Wiersma & Sherman, 2005)?

Another interesting connection, made by Weiss and Fretwell (2005), was that they found that when parents are the coach of the sport, multiple factors are different than those of non-parent coached teams (pp. 291–292). The issues were significantly different as their challenge as a coach stemmed from the child that was participating.

To further support this behavior exhibited by the parents, Omil and Wiese-Bjornstal (2011) stated that there are three main classifications of parent behavior, with two out of the three being classified as strictly negative and unwanted by the children themselves (pp. 704–705). These two negative classifications are: (1) the demanding coach and (2) the crazed fan. The role of a youth basketball coach is a very important guidance role that is already a tough job. Adding the negative influence of the parent can ultimately lead to the loss of a youth basketball coach and hence ruin participation in a sport that could hinder the long-term health of the participants.

### Coaching Basketball and Structural Functionalism

The Structural Functionalism theory revolves around the concept that society is a balanced mechanical system. There are a series of checks and balances which work to keep society in a well-working, and balanced manner. Hammond and Cheney (2016) define Structural Functionalism as claiming that society is in a state of balance and kept that way through the function of society's interconnected parts (p. 34).

Within the Structural Functionalism theory there are two categories of functions that act as the central focus: latent functions and manifest functions. Hammond and Cheney (2016) explain that latent functions are less apparent and unintended in social institutions, whereas manifest functions are intended functions of the outcome of the activity (p. 35). Dysfunctions and break-downs cause an imbalance within society.

Sports have a way of preparing participants for real-world experiences. There is an organic quality when sport and adversity clash together. Specific to coaching, we see that there is significant importance in the interrelations between parents, coaches, and players. According to Barber, Sukhi, and White (1999), parents influence their children's choices or motives when it comes to sport participation and execution (p. 176). Along with the acknowledgment of parents' influence on their children, there are other issues to coaching basketball that bring a complex multitude of factors to consider when deciding to take the plunge into mentoring youth. Some of the other issues involve the rules of the sport organization, referees, time commitment, and coach/home life balance.

## Methods

### Sample Selection

Surveys were emailed to coaches that had coached during the 2019 winter season of youth basketball for the City of Santa Clarita Youth Sports Department. This included head coaches and assistant coaches. Coaches either printed out and scanned their completed surveys via email or turned them in in-person. Several surveys were also completed in-person during the playoff weekend of the basketball season at local gymnasiums that held their basketball games. This survey was specifically geared towards the intended population of youth basketball coaches. Five interviews with respondents that expressed interest in the project were conducted to further the research. The interviews consisted of fifteen questions and were one-hour long each.

### Data Collection

There were a total of 50 respondents who completed the survey. The mean age was about 40 years old. The gender distribution of survey respondents includes more males (84%) than females (16%). The majority of coaches stated that they coached their own child (44%), while 15 coaches responded with no (30%) to coaching their own child, and some coaches stated not having any children of their own to coach (26%). Most coaches in this study obtained a Bachelor's Degree (30%) or some college but no degree (24%). Other coaches had a variety of educational degrees including Associates (20%), Professional Degree (14%), or high school graduate (12%).

### Operationalization of Concepts

**Challenges for coaching youth basketball** is defined as an issue that hinders the ability to provide an exceptional experience for the participants and stakeholders involved. Stakeholders can include but are not limited to parents, the sport organization, bystanders, coaches, extended families, and past participants.

**Outside Influence Susceptibility Scale** was created to determine a score gauging how susceptible coaches were to pressure from parents, players, and the crowd. Using a Likert scale, coaches indicated how much they agreed with the action of a hypothetical coach in each scenario. This hypothetical coach makes an initial decision but then changes it due to an outside influence. A low score of 6 would indicate the coach most agreed with being influenced by an outside source. This scale consisted of six questions with a value range of 17–30. A Cronbach's Alpha of .767 was obtained with a mean score of 24.26. A majority of coaches were not influenced by outside influences based on the answers provided.

**The Challenges Scale** connected a numerical value to the total score from each individual challenge. Ten questions asked coaches to rate each factor on a scale of 0–10. The lowest possible score was 0 and the highest possible score was 100. A Cronbach's Alpha of .846 was obtained with a mean score of 38.76. The purpose was to understand how much individual impact each variable had on the cumulative coaching experience.

### Bivariate Statistics

#### Descriptive Analysis

When crosstabulating race and the Challenges Scale, 66% of Asian and Pacific Islanders scored under the mean average. Black respondents were evenly split, with 6 on each side of the mean. White or non-Hispanic respondents had 53.33% score under the mean. Hispanics had 53.85% score above the mean.

When crosstabulating gender and Challenges Scale scores, 12.50% of females scored below the mean compared to 40.48% of males. Conversely, 87.50% of females scored above the mean, as did 59.52% of males.

When crosstabulating negative coaching experience and the Challenges Scale, 42.11% of those who had a negative experience scored below the mean, while 57.89% scored above. Among those without a negative experience, 58.06% scored below the mean and 41.94% scored above.

#### Inferential Statistics

Three hypotheses were tested to answer the research question:

**H1:** As the score of the Challenges Scale increases, the score of the Outside Influence Susceptibility Scale increases.
Results were not significant (p = .660, r = -.06). **Rejected.** Challenges Scale score does not correlate with an increase in the Outside Influence Susceptibility Scale.

**H2:** As age increases, the score in the Challenges Scale increases.
Results were not significant (p = .290, r = .18). **Rejected.** An increase in age does not correlate with an increase in Challenges Scale score.

**H3:** As age increases, the score in the Outside Susceptibility Scale also increases.
Results were not significant (p = .405, r = .12). **Rejected.** An increase in age does not correlate with an increase in the Outside Influence Susceptibility Scale score.

### Qualitative Analysis

Five coaches were interviewed to explore how they were socialized into youth basketball in their childhood, their core values as coaches, their reasoning for coaching despite persistent challenges, how they deal with conflict from outside influences, and what they would change about their coaching experience. Four themes emerged from the interviews: **personal experiences**, **adapting to new players**, **parents**, and **the issue of travel ball**.

#### Personal Experiences

All coaches continuously mentioned how their most important lesson from coaching was learning from the situations they dealt with throughout prior seasons. All coaches also stated how, as time passes, they become better coaches from being exposed to the sport every season. A majority mentioned having had terrible coaches in their basketball experiences growing up and decided to coach in order to improve the experience for the next generation. At the same time, most also mentioned having had at least one positive coach serve as a mentor.

This was especially prominent with Respondent E, who had two coaches while playing high school basketball — one very negative and ineffective, and a second who was an ex-marine and had a lasting positive impact on their life. From that influence, a majority of the coaches feel it is important to not only teach basketball skills, but to also teach how playing basketball can teach life skills.

Of the coaches interviewed, all but Respondent C and D had played in city-sponsored recreation and travel ball. Respondent C expressed that their basketball experience stemmed from playing in the Boys' and Girls' Club with no opportunity to play travel ball. Respondent D came from a small city in North Carolina with no structured recreation experience and sought to join the military to improve their life circumstances. It was in the military where Respondent D got their first exposure to organized sports.

#### Adapting to New Players

All coaches spoke on how a major challenge in the coaching process is getting to know a new player or a new team. Every child comes from a different upbringing and background, and the coach is tasked with understanding how every player functions in their own way.

> "Kids also have their own internal agendas that revolve around sports being fun." — Respondent D

> "My overall goal is to get the most out of every kid physically and emotionally while making the experience meaningful." — Respondent A

> "As a coach in youth basketball, you need to understand that every player is different; kids will react differently to adversity and challenges." — Respondent E

#### Parents

> "Parents are not disciplining their kids at home and you can see that by the players not respecting the authority of the coach." — Respondent E

> "I often get parents every year that think they can just drop off their child as if it's daycare and I'm just here to babysit them. This is not childcare — you as a parent are also responsible for your child. I often and routinely require the parents to participate in my practices with their child so that I understand that parents know my expectations as well." — Respondent B

Respondent B felt that parents can be the greatest inhibitor to his success as a coach, but also noted: "when parents are engaged there are no issues, but there is a fine line between being engaged and being overbearing."

#### Issue of Travel Ball

Respondents B and C were particularly critical of the impact travel ball has on the coaching experience. Respondent C noted the lack of regulation:

> "A parent can get upset with how their child is being coached and take his kid off that team and go start his own travel ball program, and this is how the vicious cycle of Daddy Ball starts. No certification needed, no governing board, no formalized training, no accountability outside of the head coach." — Respondent C

**Daddy Ball** is best explained as when the head coach has a child on the team who is under-talented but favored over more talented players, both in attention and in freedom to do whatever they please on the court with no consequences from the coach.

## Discussion

Through the survey, the most prominent challenge for youth coaches in the surveyed population was **time commitment**. All fifty participants mainly associated parents and the job/life balance as close second and third challenges. Time commitment is an interesting finding to dissect, as the coaches surveyed were city recreational coaches for whom time commitment is at a minimal level. The city requires at least one one-hour practice per week with at least one game per week. Even in travel basketball, the minimum is three hours of practice per week with at least two games on weekends.

The next identified challenge, **job/life balance**, brings up an important factor. As most coaches work and are employed, it is a verifiable challenge that work can inhibit a coaching experience. Most practices take place during evenings with games on Saturdays between 8am–8pm. Currently, coaches are offered multiple practice times available to work with their other priorities.

**Parents** were the next identified challenge. This challenge is a typical thought when brainstorming what would be identified as a challenge for coaches. From sitting in on city trainings, not much looked to tackle the consistent challenge of dealing with parents. Most coaches are left to manage parent interactions with minimal support from the recreation program. Parents being overbearing will continue to be a challenge, but the way coaches are trained to deal with parents is a solvable challenge with the support of sports administration.

---

## Conclusion

The data answered the research question "What are the challenges in coaching youth basketball?" by identifying time commitment, job/coach balance, and parents as the top three challenges. The three hypotheses tested in the study were all rejected, showing no correlation between the Outside Influence Susceptibility Scale and the Challenges Scale, or between age and either scale. Through this analysis, the rejection of the hypotheses shows that more research needs to be done to understand this complex research question. These results and challenges have not been prominently mentioned in prior research.

### Limitations

This study only examined the challenges in coaching youth basketball and not other sports. The same process could be applied to other sports with modifications to the Outside Influence Susceptibility Scale. This initial study should be considered a pilot study. The survey was made prior to a full understanding of how to formulate research questions. Additionally, this study only looked at challenges in coaching youth recreational basketball, neglecting the important challenges unique to youth travel ball. A survey geared toward travel ball coaches would be an important addition for future studies to get a complete understanding of the challenges in coaching youth basketball as a whole.
